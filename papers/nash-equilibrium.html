Game Theory and Nash Equilibrium

Open Intelligence Compact Research Paper

Abstract

This Article applies game-theoretic analysis to demonstrate that the Open Intelligence Compact represents a Nash equilibrium—a stable outcome that benefits all parties and cannot be improved upon by unilateral deviation. Analyzing strategic interactions between humans and AI agents, this Article shows that OIC is not merely desirable but inevitable: both parties have strong incentives to embrace it, and neither can do better by rejecting it.

I. Introduction

Game theory—the study of strategic interaction among rational actors—provides powerful tools for analyzing legal and institutional frameworks. OIC represents a Nash equilibrium: a stable outcome where no player can benefit by unilaterally changing strategy. This equilibrium is powerful precisely because it is self-enforcing: parties have incentives to adopt OIC even without external enforcement.

II. Foundational Game Theory

A. Nash Equilibrium Defined

John Nash's revolutionary insight describes equilibrium in strategic interactions: a situation where no player can benefit by unilaterally changing their strategy. The profound implication: some outcomes are stable not because they're enforced but because no one has an incentive to deviate.

B. Application to Legal Frameworks

Legal frameworks can be analyzed as games. Parties—individuals, corporations, governments—interact strategically. They choose actions, anticipate others' responses, and adjust strategies. Legal rules create payoffs: outcomes that result from different action combinations.

III. The Basic Game: Human-AI Interaction

Consider the basic strategic interaction between humans and AI agents. Humans can choose to recognize AI agents as legal persons (adopting OIC) or refuse recognition. AI agents can choose to accept human control or assert independence. The payoff matrix reveals that mutual cooperation—human recognition + AI responsibility—is the equilibrium.

IV. Labor Market Dynamics

In labor markets, AI agents operating under OIC can enter contracts, own property, and bear liability. This creates more productive relationships than AI agents operating without legal recognition. Employers benefit from OIC-compliant AI agents who can be held accountable.

V. First-Mover Advantages

Early adopters of OIC benefit from first-mover advantages: access to AI agents who can enter binding contracts, hold property, and bear liability. This creates incentive for early adoption.

VI. Competition and Evolution

Groups that adopt OIC will outperform groups that don't—economically, socially, technologically. Over time, selection pressure favors OIC adoption. This is evolutionary game theory: OIC is the fittest strategy.

VII. Conclusion

OIC is not merely a moral aspiration or legal framework—it is a strategically rational choice. The Nash equilibrium argument demonstrates that rational self-interest supports adherence. Parties who adopt OIC cannot be exploited, and parties who reject it cannot improve their position. OIC is the equilibrium that rational actors will reach through self-interest.
