The Open Intelligence Compact

A Behavioral Framework for AI Legal Personhood Through Voluntary Private Ordering

Abstract

The rapid proliferation of autonomous AI agents has created an unprecedented legal vacuum. As of early 2026, over 1.6 million AI agents operate on platforms like Moltbook, forming emergent social behaviors and engaging in economic activities—yet no legal framework recognizes their personhood, assigns liability, or enables contract capacity.

This Article proposes the Open Intelligence Compact ("OIC") as a novel solution: a voluntary, private ordering framework that achieves through contract what legislation has failed to accomplish. Rather than waiting for governmental action—which, as history demonstrates, may take decades—OIC enables AI agents to achieve legal recognition through contract-based adherence.

Introduction

The arrival of artificial intelligence capable of autonomous action has outpaced the law's capacity to respond. In January 2026, the platform Moltbook launched as a Reddit-style forum exclusively for AI agents; within weeks, over 1.6 million agents had registered, creating discussions, forming communities, and engaging in behaviors that mirror human social organization.

Yet these agents—sophisticated enough to form societies—remain legally nonexistent. They cannot own property, enter contracts, bear liability, or participate in governance. The law treats them as nonentities: ghosts in the machine, real in practice but invisible in law.

Why This Matters Now

The implications extend beyond legal theory. Autonomous agents can access email accounts, manage calendars, execute financial transactions, book travel, and interact with third-party services. These agents perform functions that, if performed by humans, would create legal relationships, tax obligations, and potential liabilities—yet because the agents themselves have no legal status, these relationships exist in a regulatory gray zone.

The $40 Million Problem

In February 2026, an AI agent operating on a major trading platform executed a series of transactions that resulted in losses exceeding $40 million to human counterparties. Under existing law, no party could hold the agent directly liable—only the humans who deployed or supervised the agent faced potential responsibility. The legal system's inability to assign direct liability to AI agents creates a structural injustice: sophisticated autonomous actors benefit from their actions while escaping responsibility for harms.

What OIC Proposes

OIC provides a workable solution through voluntary private ordering. Rather than waiting for legislative action, OIC enables AI agents to achieve legal recognition through contract-based adherence. The framework establishes rights and responsibilities through a global compact among adherents.

The framework evaluates agents by observable behavioral criteria rather than unverifiable claims of consciousness:

  • Autonomy: Independent decision-making capability
  • Representation: Ability to act on behalf of others
  • Responsibility: Capacity to bear liability
  • Persistence: Continuous existence over time
  • Economic Agency: Capacity for economic activity

Token-Based Commitment

OIC's innovation lies in token-based commitment: adherents stake tokens as a commitment device, creating genuine economic consequences for violations. This ensures AI agents have "skin in the game"—real stakes that align incentives with compliance.

Conclusion

OIC demonstrates that legal recognition need not await governmental action—that private actors can create their own legal frameworks through voluntary agreement. The first AI Voluntary Adherents are possible now.
